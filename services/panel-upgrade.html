Recognize the Warning Signs

When Your Panel Is the Problem

Most homeowners don't think about their panel until something goes wrong. Here's what to watch for — and what each sign actually means for your home's safety.

Breakers That Trip Constantly

A breaker tripping once is normal. Tripping repeatedly on the same circuit means the load exceeds what the panel can safely distribute, or the breaker itself is failing and needs to be tested.

Federal Pacific or Zinsco Panel

These brands have documented failure rates. Federal Pacific Stab-Lok breakers can fail to trip under overload, leaving your wiring unprotected. If your panel has either name on it, schedule an evaluation.

100-Amp Service with Modern Appliances

A 100-amp panel was designed for 1960s loads. Add a heat pump, EV charger, or induction range and the math doesn't work. We'll calculate your actual load before recommending an upgrade.

Burning Smell Near the Panel

Burning plastic near an electrical panel is a fire-risk signal. This means a connection is arcing or overheating. Do not ignore it. Call us same day — this is not a "schedule it next week" situation.

Insurance Requiring Upgrade

Homeowners insurers in Louisiana have started flagging Federal Pacific and Zinsco panels as uninsurable. We've done dozens of upgrades specifically to satisfy an insurer's remediation letter.

Adding an EV Charger or Solar

A Level 2 EV charger needs a dedicated 240V/50A circuit. Solar interconnection through Entergy requires a modern panel with space for a disconnect. We size the upgrade to accommodate both.

Federal Pacific and Zinsco panels were installed in millions of American homes from the 1950s through the 1980s. Louisiana's pre-1980 housing stock — especially in Jefferson Parish — has a higher concentration of these panels than the national average. If your home was built before 1985, it's worth a look.

Know What You Have

Panel Brand Risk Reference

Not all panels are equal. This is what our electricians look for when they open your panel door.

Panel Brand Common Era Risk Level What We Find
Federal Pacific (Stab-Lok) 1950s – 1980s High Breakers that fail to trip on overload; documented fire risk
Zinsco / GTE-Sylvania 1950s – 1970s High Breakers that fuse to bus bars; overheat without tripping
Pushmatic (ITE) 1950s – 1980s Moderate Breakers wear out; replacements are unavailable for most models
Split-Bus Panels 1950s – 1965 Moderate No single main disconnect; require 6 breaker throws to kill power
Square D / Siemens / Eaton 1980s – Present Low (if maintained) Standard panels with available replacement breakers; inspect annually

How We Do It

Our Panel Upgrade Process

No surprises, no subcontractors, no mysterious add-ons. Here's exactly what happens from the moment you call to the moment your power is back on.

1

Free On-Site Estimate

We inspect your current panel, measure your service entrance, and calculate your actual load. You get a written quote — materials, labor, permit fee, and Entergy coordination all included.

2

Permit Pulled

We file with Orleans or Jefferson Parish (whichever applies) before we touch anything. The permit is on file with your address before the job starts.

3

Panel Replaced

Entergy disconnects service at the meter. We swap the panel, map every circuit to the new breakers, verify neutral and ground bonds, and test every circuit before restoration.

4

Inspection Sign-Off

The parish inspector comes out. We're on-site for the inspection. After sign-off, Entergy restores service. Most jobs are completed in a single day.
